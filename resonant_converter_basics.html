Mastering Resonant Converters

A conceptual and analytical guide from LC tanks to ZVS design.

1. The LC Tank: Why is it Sinusoidal?

A resonant converter's heart is the LC tank. While a DAB (Dual Active Bridge) is essentially a purely inductive converter, a resonant converter adds a capacitor in series.

The Harmonic Filter

We apply a Square Wave (from the MOSFET bridge) to the tank. A square wave contains the fundamental frequency and all odd harmonics (3rd, 5th, etc.).

The LC tank is a Band-Pass Filter. At the resonant frequency fr = 1 / (2π√(LC)), the impedance of the L and C cancel each other out. Higher harmonics see a very high impedance and are "filtered out," leaving only the fundamental Sine Wave.

The tank filters harmonics into a pure sine wave.

Why is DAB Trapezoidal?
DAB uses only an Inductor (L). In an inductor, V = L(di/dt). When you apply a constant DC voltage from a bridge, the current changes linearly (di = V/L dt). These linear ramps create the trapezoidal/triangular shapes you see in DAB.

2. Impedance and the "Inductive Region"

To achieve Soft Switching (ZVS), the tank must look Inductive to the bridge. This depends on your switching frequency (fsw) relative to resonance (fr).

  • Inductive Region (fsw > fr): The current lags the voltage. This is our target for ZVS.
  • Capacitive Region (fsw < fr): The current leads the voltage. This leads to hard-switching and diode recovery issues.

ZVS Mechanism (Inductive Load)

Why Inductive? In the inductive region, when a MOSFET turns OFF, the current is still flowing in the same direction. This current charges the Coss of the turning-off switch and discharges the Coss of the complementary switch. When the voltage across the next switch hits zero, its body diode conducts, and we turn it on at Zero Voltage.

3. Comparing Topologies

LLC Converter (The Gold Standard)

Uses two inductors (Lr resonant, Lm magnetizing) and one capacitor (Cr).

Benefit: Can maintain ZVS from zero to full load. High efficiency. Uses primary MOSFETs and secondary Diodes (or Sync-Rects).

DAB - Dual Active Bridge

Two active MOSFET bridges connected via a transformer and a series inductor.

Benefit: Bidirectional by nature. Control is via Phase Shift between bridges rather than frequency modulation.

CLLC (Bidirectional LLC)

A symmetric version of the LLC with resonant tanks on both primary and secondary.

Benefit: Optimized for bidirectional chargers (like EVs) where efficiency is critical in both directions.

4. How L and C are actually sized

Your friend is right that many people "guess," but world-class engineers use the First Harmonic Approximation (FHA).

Step 1: Choose Resonant Frequency (fr)

This is your "sweet spot." Usually 100kHz - 500kHz depending on your MOSFET technology (GaN/SiC allows higher).

Step 2: Define the Quality Factor (Q)

Q = (√(Lr/Cr)) / Rload

A higher Q makes the gain curve sharper (narrow frequency range, but harder to control). A lower Q makes it flatter. Typical design target: 0.4 to 0.6.

Step 3: The Inductance Ratio (Ln)

Ln = Lm / Lr

Usually between 3 and 7. Lower values give a wider gain range but increase circulating current (lower efficiency).

Intuition: Lr and Cr set the frequency, while their ratio sets how "stiff" the tank is against load changes.

5. Dead Time and Coss: The Physics

Soft switching isn't "hope"; it's a balance of energy. Every MOSFET has an output capacitance (Coss). To achieve ZVS, the Energy stored in the Inductor must be greater than the Energy in the Capacitors.

The Energy Condition

½ Lm Imag² > 2 × ½ Coss Vin²

The magnetizing energy must be enough to "flip" the voltage of both MOSFETs in a leg.

The Timing Condition

tdead ≥ (2 · Vin · Coss) / Imag

The dead time must be long enough for the current to actually finish discharging the capacitor.

If tdead is too short, the MOSFET turns on while there is still voltage across it (Hard Switching). If tdead is too long, the body diode conducts too long, or the voltage might even start "swinging back" up, losing the ZVS window.
