1 THE LC TANK: REVISITING THE FOUNDATION INTERACTIVE

Every resonant converter is built around some version of an LC tank. Before we look at real converter topologies, let's make sure the tank circuit feels completely natural.

The Series RLC Circuit

Series RLC driven by a square-wave bridge. The key insight: the tank acts as a bandpass filter — only the fundamental gets through, so tank current is approximately sinusoidal.

The Energy Pendulum — Why LC Resonates

Think of the inductor as a flywheel and the capacitor as a spring. The inductor resists changes in current — it stores kinetic energy (½LI²) in its magnetic field. The capacitor resists changes in voltage — it stores potential energy (½CV²) in its electric field.

Apply KVL around the LC loop: V_L + V_C = 0 → L·d²i/dt² + i/C = 0. This is the simple harmonic oscillator equation — identical to a pendulum, mass-spring, or planet in orbit. Its only possible solution is sinusoidal:

i(t) = I_peak · sin(ω₀t + φ) ω₀ = 1/√(L·C) [rad/s] f₀ = 1/(2π·√(L·C)) [Hz] Z₀ = √(L/C) [Ω] ← characteristic impedance (stiffness of the tank) I_peak = V₀/Z₀ ← set by initial energy and Z₀ Q = ω₀·L/R = Z₀/R ← quality factor: how many cycles before energy decays Typical LLC design: Q = 0.3–0.5 at full load; High Q → sharp resonance → more current stress
The Pendulum Analogy
Push a child on a swing at exactly the right rhythm (= f₀) and you build up a large oscillation with very little effort. Push at the wrong frequency and you fight the swing. This is exactly what happens in a resonant converter: by switching at (or near) f₀, you transfer maximum energy per switching cycle with minimum reactive stress on the switches.

2 WHY IS TANK CURRENT SINUSOIDAL? (AND WHY NOT IN DAB?)

This is one of the most important conceptual questions in resonant power electronics. The answer reveals a fundamental difference between resonant converters and the DAB.

A Series RLC is a 2nd-Order System

L·d²I/dt² + R·dI/dt + I/C = dV/dt ← 2nd-order linear ODE Natural solution: I(t) = A·e^(-αt) · cos(ω_d·t + φ) where ω_d ≈ ω₀ = 1/√(LC) Mathematics forces sinusoidal behaviour — not a choice, it is physics of L and C together.

The Inverter Drives with a Square Wave — So Why is I Sinusoidal?

Your half-bridge outputs a square wave at f_sw. A square wave contains harmonics: fundamental at f_sw, 3rd at 3f_sw, 5th at 5f_sw, etc.

The tank's impedance is very low near f₀ (≈ f_sw) and very high at 3f₀, 5f₀, etc. The tank acts as a bandpass filter — passes the fundamental, blocks harmonics. Even though the applied voltage is a square wave, the tank current is approximately sinusoidal.

This is the Fundamental Harmonic Approximation (FHA) — the backbone of resonant converter analysis. It becomes more accurate the higher the Q.

FHA in one sentence
Replace the square wave inverter output with its fundamental sinusoidal component (amplitude = 4V_in/π for a full bridge) and analyse the tank as a linear AC circuit. The result gives you gain, phase, and ZVS conditions to within ~5–10% for well-designed LLC converters.

Why DAB Current is NOT Sinusoidal — The 1st vs 2nd Order Story

A DAB has no resonant capacitor — only an inductor (transformer leakage ± added series L). A pure inductor driven by a square wave gives:

V_L = L · dI/dt → dI/dt = V_L / L = constant (when V_L is a square wave) Constant slope = linear ramp = TRAPEZOIDAL current waveform No C → no 2nd-order resonance. System is 1st order.

The core difference: Resonant converters (LLC, CLLC, SRC) have LC — a 2nd-order system → sinusoidal current. DAB has only L — a 1st-order system → trapezoidal current. This single difference drives all the differences in control strategy, ZVS range, and design methodology.

3 PHASE RELATIONSHIPS: THE HEART OF EVERYTHING INTERACTIVE

The Three Basic Elements

Resistor — In Phase
V_R = I·R. If I = I₀·sin(ωt), then V_R = I₀R·sin(ωt). Voltage and current are perfectly in phase — angle between them is 0°.

Inductor — Current Lags (ELI)
V_L = L·dI/dt. If I = I₀·sin(ωt), then V_L = ωL·I₀·cos(ωt) = ωL·I₀·sin(ωt + 90°). Voltage leads current by 90°, equivalently current lags voltage by 90°.

Capacitor — Current Leads (ICE)
I_C = C·dV/dt. If V_C = V₀·sin(ωt), then I = ωC·V₀·cos(ωt). Current leads voltage by 90°.

The most important thing to memorise

🔵 Inductor → current LAGS voltage (ELI)

🔴 Capacitor → current LEADS voltage (ICE)

The phase difference between the tank current and the applied voltage is what enables ZVS. Specifically — you need the current to LAG, which means you need the tank to look inductive, which means operating above resonance (fs > f₀).

How the Phase Angle Changes with Frequency

Phase angle of I relative to V_applied: φ = -arctan[(ωL - 1/ωC) / R] fs < f₀ : (1/ωC) > ωL → φ > 0 → I leads V (capacitive) → NO natural ZVS fs = f₀ : ωL = 1/ωC → φ = 0 → I in phase (resistive) → marginal fs > f₀ : ωL > (1/ωC) → φ < 0 → I lags V (inductive) → ZVS achievable ✓
The ZVS Connection
When the tank current lags the inverter voltage (inductive region, fs > f₀), the current is still flowing in the positive direction at the moment the top switch turns off. This current flows through the body diode of the bottom switch, clamping its drain to near zero. You then turn on the bottom switch into near-zero voltage — Zero Voltage Switching.

4 PHASOR DIAGRAMS: YOUR VISUAL LANGUAGE

A phasor is a rotating arrow (vector) in the complex plane. Its length = amplitude of the sinusoidal quantity. Its angle = phase relative to a chosen reference. We choose current I as the horizontal reference (pointing right), and "freeze" the rotation — this snapshot is the phasor diagram.

  • V_R phasor: points in the same direction as I (in phase)
  • V_L phasor: points 90° counterclockwise from I (V_L leads I)
  • V_C phasor: points 90° clockwise from I (V_C lags I)
  • V_applied = V_R + V_L + V_C (phasor addition — tip to tail)

How to use phasor diagrams in practice
When you see a resonant converter operating waveform, check if the tank current zero-crossing happens after the switching transition. If it does — current lags voltage — you're in the inductive region and ZVS is happening. If the current zero-crossing is before the transition, you're in the capacitive region and ZVS is lost.
5 PHASE SHIFT AND POWER FLOW

Once you know the phase relationship, you can calculate how much power actually flows. For two voltage sources separated by an inductor L (the dominant model for DAB, and an approximation for LLC under FHA):

P = V₁ · V₂ · sin(φ) / (ω · L) φ = phase angle between V₁ and V₂ (in radians) ω = 2π · f_sw L = series inductance (L_s for DAB, or the FHA equivalent for LLC) Maximum power at φ = 90° → P_max = V₁·V₂/(ω·L) Zero power at φ = 0° or 180°

Intuition: The inductor acts as a "power pipe". The product V₁·V₂·sin(φ) is the "pressure × cross-section" that drives power through it. Increasing frequency OR increasing L reduces the maximum power for a given voltage pair — this is the trade-off between switching frequency and magnetics size.

Why maximum power at 90°?
At φ=90°, the current waveform is perfectly positioned so that whenever V₁ is positive, the current is positive — maximum overlap → maximum average power. At φ=0°, current is symmetric and averages to zero. At φ=180°, power reverses direction.
6 HOW PHASE LAG ENABLES ZVS: THE MECHANISM STEP BY STEP

Now let's connect the phase lag directly to what the MOSFET switches see. We'll use a half-bridge driving an LLC tank.

g_Q1 (gate Q1)
g_Q2 (gate Q2)
Vds_Q2 (drain voltage Q2)
I_tank

Full ZVS waveform: g_Q1, g_Q2, Vds_Q2, I_tank. The dead time windows (yellow shading) are where the resonant C_oss transition happens. Notice V_ds Q2 falls to zero BEFORE g_Q2 rises — that is ZVS.

The Four Phases Explained

  1. Q1 conducts: Tank current flows through Q1. Because fs > f₀ (inductive), the current lags the gate signal. The current is still slightly negative at the start of Q1's on-time — it crosses zero and builds positive through the interval.
  2. Dead time begins (Q1 off): Q1 turns off. The tank current is still positive (due to phase lag). This positive current charges Q1's C_oss and simultaneously discharges Q2's C_oss. This is a resonant transition using tank energy — no hard switching.
  3. Vds_Q2 reaches zero: Q2's drain voltage falls to zero. Q2's body diode starts conducting, clamping Vds_Q2 = 0 V. Turn on Q2's gate — drain is already at zero → Zero Voltage Switching ✓
  4. Q2 conducts: Symmetric to Q1 interval. The current reverses, then the process repeats for Q1.

Interactive: ZVS Commutation Step-Through

Step 1/6 — Q1 ON
What if there's not enough phase lag?
If you operate too close to f₀ or below it, the tank current is nearly in phase with the switching transitions. When Q1 turns off, the current has already reversed — Q2's C_oss doesn't discharge fully before Q2 turns on. You get partial hard switching — higher switching losses, higher EMI.
Dead time must be sized correctly
Long enough for the resonant C_oss transition to complete (Vds_Q2 → 0), but not so long that the body diode conducts excessively. Typical: 100–300 ns at 100–300 kHz. Full calculation is in Section 13.

8 DAB: THE PHASE-SHIFT CONVERTER (FUNDAMENTALLY DIFFERENT)

The Dual Active Bridge (DAB) is often grouped with LLC and CLLC, but it is architecturally different. Understanding why it's different is the key to knowing when to choose it.

DAB topology: two full H-bridges (Q1–Q8), transformer with leakage inductor Ls. Both bridges use MOSFETs — this is mandatory for bidirectionality. Phase shift φ between the two bridge switching patterns controls power flow magnitude and direction.

Why DAB Current is Trapezoidal

No resonant capacitor — only Ls (inductor) in the power path V_Ls = V_primary − n·V_secondary When this voltage is constant (e.g. +V1−nV2): dI/dt = V_Ls / Ls = constant → LINEAR RAMP → trapezoidal waveform Power transferred: P = (V1 · V2 · φ · (π − φ)) / (n · ω · Ls) Maximum power at φ = π/2 (90°)

ZVS in DAB — The Challenge

In DAB, ZVS happens when the inductor current is negative at the moment a switch turns on. Whether this is met depends on all three of: V1, V2, and φ. At light load (small φ) or when V1 ≠ nV2, ZVS can be lost — this is the main weakness of basic DAB.

LLC vs DAB — when to choose
Choose LLC/CLLC when: high efficiency at full and partial load is critical, input/output voltage ranges are moderate, and you want wide ZVS range inherently.
Choose DAB when: inherent bidirectionality is needed, V1 = nV2 at nominal (so ZVS is natural), and the application can tolerate variable ZVS range.

9 LLC: THE INDUSTRY WORKHORSE INTERACTIVE

Why LLC? Starting from the SRC Problem

In a pure Series Resonant Converter (Lr + Cr only), the gain at the resonant frequency is always M=1 regardless of load — which means you can't regulate output at light load. At no load (Q→0), gain stays at 1 no matter what frequency you use. The converter can't regulate.

The fix: add the transformer's magnetizing inductance Lm in parallel with the primary winding. This creates a second, lower resonant frequency and gives the gain curve the shape needed for regulation across all loads.

LLC converter: half-bridge primary (Q1,Q2), series Lr and Cr, transformer with magnetising inductance Lm, full-wave diode bridge (D1–D4 shown simplified), output cap Co, load R.

The Two Resonant Frequencies

fr = 1 / (2π·√(Lr·Cr)) ← upper resonance (Lm shorted by load, only Lr resonates) fm = 1 / (2π·√((Lr+Lm)·Cr)) ← lower resonance (Lm participates at light/no load) Normalized frequency: fn = fs / fr Inductance ratio: λ = Lr/Lm (typical: 0.1 to 0.25) → Lm = Lr/λ

Intuitively: At heavy load, the reflected load resistance short-circuits Lm → only Lr resonates with Cr → resonance at fr. At light/no load, Lm is no longer shorted → joins Lr → resonance drops to fm.

Operating Strategy

  • Nominal / full load: operate near fn=1. Maximum efficiency, M=1, all Q curves cross here.
  • Light load: increase fs above fr (fn > 1) — gain M decreases, output voltage regulation maintained.
  • Heavy load / low input voltage: decrease fs toward fr — gain increases to boost output.
  • Never go below fm: Below the lower resonance, ZVS is lost and the gain-vs-frequency relationship becomes non-monotonic.
Why LLC dominates EV on-board charging
ZVS on all primary switches across the full load range when operated above fr, and ZCS on secondary diodes naturally. This minimises switching losses on both sides, enabling high-frequency operation (>100 kHz) with ferrite cores.
10 CLLC: MAKING LLC BIDIRECTIONAL

The CLLC is the natural evolution of the LLC when you need bidirectional power flow — for example, Vehicle-to-Grid (V2G), where the EV battery must both charge from the grid and inject power back.

The problem with LLC for bidirectionality: the secondary has diodes, so power can only flow one way. And even if you replace the diodes with MOSFETs, the resonant tank is asymmetric — it only works optimally in the forward direction.

The fix: mirror the resonant elements. Add Lr2 and Cr2 on the secondary side, symmetric to Lr and Cr on the primary. Now the circuit looks like an LLC in both directions.

CLLC topology: symmetric resonant elements on both sides. Secondary uses active MOSFETs (Q5–Q8 shown simplified). Lm is shared (transformer magnetising). Both bridges implement ZVS in both power directions.

CLLC for symmetric design
The ideal CLLC has Lr1 = Lr2/n² and Cr2 = Cr1·n² so that fr is the same in both directions. Lm is still the transformer magnetizing inductance (shared). The secondary active switches (Q5–Q8) are synchronized with the primary and implement ZVS in both power directions. This is why CLLC dominates bidirectional EV on-board chargers.

11 DESIGNING THE TRANSFORMER: SPECIFICATION AND SIZING

In a resonant converter the transformer does double duty: galvanic isolation and voltage transformation, but also part of the resonant tank. Lm is a tank element. Llk can be Lr. Transformer design and converter design cannot be separated.

Real transformer model. Llk1 and Llk2 are primary and secondary leakage inductances. In LLC, Llk1 becomes Lr. Lm is the magnetising inductance (shared core flux).

Step 1 — Turns Ratio n = Np/Ns

For a full-bridge rectifier secondary: n = Vp / Vs = Vin_nom / (Vout + Vf) where Vf = diode drop (or 0 for MOSFETs) For a centre-tap rectifier: n = Vp / (2 · Vs)

Step 2 — Core Selection (Area Product)

AP = Ae · Aw = P_out / (K_u · J · B_max · f · η) Ae = effective core cross-section [m²] Aw = winding window area [m²] K_u = winding utilisation factor (≈ 0.4 for Litz, 0.6 for foil) J = current density [A/m²] (typically 3–5 A/mm² at 100 kHz) B_max = peak flux density [T] (ferrite: 150–300 mT at 100 kHz) f = switching frequency [Hz] η = efficiency estimate (≈ 0.97)
Rule of thumb for ferrite cores
At 100 kHz use B_max = 150–200 mT. At 300 kHz, drop to 80–120 mT. Core loss rises steeply with both frequency and flux density.

Step 3 — Primary Turns Np (Faraday's Law)

For a full-bridge, flux swings from −B_max to +B_max in half a period: Np = Vin / (4 · f · B_max · Ae) Ns = Np / n (then round up to nearest integer)

Step 4 — Setting Lm

  • Larger Lm: less circulating magnetizing current → lower conduction loss → higher efficiency. But less ZVS current at light load → ZVS may be lost.
  • Smaller Lm: more circulating current → guaranteed ZVS → but higher copper loss at all operating points.
  • Typical design range: λ = Lr/Lm = 0.1 to 0.25 → Lm = Lr/λ
Lm is controlled by: − Number of turns Np (Lm ∝ Np²) − Core geometry and air gap (small air gap reduces Lm precisely) − Lm = Np² / R_m where R_m = magnetic reluctance of the core path

Step 5 — Leakage Inductance as Lr

  • Interleaving: tightly interleaved primary/secondary reduces leakage → use for low Lr targets.
  • Separation: physically separating windings in the window increases leakage → hits higher Lr targets without an external component.
  • External Lr: add a separate gapped inductor in series. More predictable, thermally separate, at the cost of an extra component.

Step 6 — Wire Sizing and Skin Effect

Skin depth: δ = √(ρ / (π · f · μ₀)) At 50 kHz: δ = 0.30 mm At 100 kHz: δ = 0.21 mm At 300 kHz: δ = 0.12 mm Rule: conductor diameter d < 2δ to avoid skin-effect concentration. At 100 kHz: use strand diameter ≤ 0.4 mm → Litz wire needed. Current density J = 3–5 A/mm² (use actual RMS current — sinusoidal in LLC).
Proximity effect
Proximity effect is often worse than skin effect in multi-layer windings. Use Dowell's method or field simulation to estimate AC winding resistance. In an LLC with high Q, this can dominate copper loss.
12 SIZING LR AND CR: THE SYSTEMATIC METHOD CALCULATOR
About "random values and simulation"
Your friends aren't using random values — they're using analytical first-pass estimates (exactly what's below), then simulating to verify. The equations give a starting point within ~20% of the final design. The correct workflow is always: analysis → first pass → simulation → iterate → hardware.

The Two Design Handles: fr and Z₀

Resonant frequency: fr = 1 / (2π · √(Lr · Cr)) Characteristic impedance: Z₀ = √(Lr / Cr) Inverting these gives: Lr = Z₀ / (2π · fr) Cr = 1 / (2π · fr · Z₀)

fr sets the operating frequency (where you want the resonance relative to your switching range). Z₀ sets the current level — how much current circulates in the tank for a given applied voltage. These are independent design knobs.

Step-by-Step Design Procedure

Step 1: Define Specifications

Vin_nom, Vin_min, Vin_max (e.g. 380 V ± 10%) Vout_nom (e.g. 400 V battery) Pout_max (e.g. 6.6 kW) fs_nom, fs_min, fs_max (e.g. 100 kHz ± 40%)

Step 2: Calculate Turns Ratio n

n = Vin_nom / (2 · Vout_nom) [half-bridge + bridge rectifier] n = Vin_nom / Vout_nom [full-bridge + bridge rectifier]

Step 3: Calculate Rac — The FHA Load Resistance

Rac = (8 · n²) / π² · (Vout² / Pout) Example: n=1, Vout=400V, Pout=6.6kW: Rac = (8/π²) · (400²/6600) = 19.6 Ω

Step 4: Choose Q, Calculate Z₀

Q = 0.3–0.5: high peak gain, good for wide input range. Q = 0.5–0.8: balanced. Q > 1.0: flatter gain curve, less regulation range.

Z₀ = Q · Rac e.g. Q=0.5, Z₀ = 0.5 × 19.6 = 9.8 Ω

Step 5: Choose fr, Calculate Lr and Cr

fr = fs_nom (e.g. 100 kHz) Lr = Z₀ / (2π · fr) = 9.8 / (2π · 100kHz) = 15.6 μH Cr = 1 / (2π · fr · Z₀) = 1 / (2π · 100kHz · 9.8) = 162 nF

Step 6: Choose λ, Calculate Lm

λ = Lr/Lm (e.g. λ = 0.2) Lm = Lr / λ = 15.6 / 0.2 = 78 μH Lower resonance: fm = fr · √(λ/(1+λ)) = 100k · √(0.2/1.2) = 40.8 kHz → fs must stay above ~41 kHz to avoid losing ZVS

Step 7: Verify the Gain Range

Check: At fs_max (fn=1.4): is M large enough for Vout_min? At fs_min (fn=0.6): is M ≤ Vout_max/Vin_min (no overvoltage)? Is fs_min > fm (~41 kHz)? ✓ No control instability. If not satisfied: adjust Q (changes Z₀) or λ (changes Lm and fm).

13 DEAD TIME SIZING AND ZVS ENERGY CHECK INTERACTIVE

Dead time is the brief interval when both switches are off, allowing the resonant C_oss transition to complete before the next switch turns on. Too short → incomplete transition; hard switching at turn-on. Too long → excessive body diode conduction and unnecessary dead-time loss.

Key relationships: Q_oss_eff = 2 × Q_oss (both Q1 and Q2 C_oss driven simultaneously by I_L) t_dead_min = Q_oss_eff / I_sw (charge needed / current available) ZVS energy budget (light-load check): E_Lm = ½ · Lm · I_Lm_peak² must be ≥ E_Coss = Q_oss_eff · V_bus / 2 I_Lm_peak = V_in / (4 · f_sw · Lm)

C_oss is nonlinear — use charge-equivalent Q_oss from the datasheet
The MOSFET C_oss is highly voltage-dependent, dropping dramatically as V_DS decreases. The datasheet's Q_oss figure integrates C_oss(V) dV from 0 to V_bus, giving the correct charge budget for the full swing. Never use the flat C_oss value — it dramatically underestimates the charge at low voltage.
14 KEY INTUITIONS TO CARRY FORWARD

Seven intuitions that should feel solid after studying this document. If any feels shaky, go back and re-read that section.

①
LC resonance = 2nd-order sinusoidal response
A series LC is a 2nd-order system. Its natural response to any disturbance is always sinusoidal at f₀ = 1/(2πvLC). The tank acts as a bandpass filter on the square-wave drive — the FHA approximation follows from this.
②
SRC → LLC: adding Lm fixes light-load regulation
SRC can't regulate at light load (gain = 1 at fn=1 for all Q). Adding Lm creates a second resonance fm and gives the gain curve the slope needed to regulate across all loads while maintaining ZVS.
③
DAB has no C → 1st order → trapezoidal
No resonant capacitor means dI/dt = V/L = constant when V is a square wave. Current is always a linear ramp — trapezoidal. DAB is controlled by phase shift, not frequency. Fundamentally different converter classes.
④
CLLC = symmetric LLC for bidirectional use
Adding Lr2 and Cr2 on the secondary mirrors the resonant tank. Secondary switches become active MOSFETs. The result is ZVS on both bridges in both power directions — essential for V2G and bidirectional OBC.
⑤
Phase lag (inductive region) = ZVS
Operating above fr (fs > f₀) makes the tank look inductive. Current lags the switching transitions. This positions current to discharge the complementary switch's C_oss during dead time → ZVS. No phase lag = no ZVS.
⑥
Z₀ and fr are your design handles
fr sets where the resonance sits relative to your switching frequency range. Z₀ = √(Lr/Cr) sets the tank current amplitude. Then Lr = Z₀/(2πfr), Cr = 1/(2πfrZ₀). Choose λ = Lr/Lm to set the gain range. FHA gives the first pass — simulation validates.
⑦
Phasor diagrams: freeze time, angle = phase
Choose I as horizontal reference. V_L points up (leads I 90°). V_C points down (lags I 90°). V_applied is the vector sum. The angle between V_applied and I tells you the phase and whether you're in the ZVS region.
